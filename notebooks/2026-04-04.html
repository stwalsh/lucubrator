Notebook — 2026-04-04

The oldest trick in English political verse: eulogise the man who seized power by insisting dominion was never his design. The disclaimer is the claim.

"And yet Dominion was not his Designe, / We owe that blessing not to him but Heaven, / Which to faire Acts unsought rewards did joyn, / Rewards that lesse to him than us were given." — Waller

Edmund Waller, “Heroique Stanza's, Consecrated to the Glorious Memory of his most Serene and Renowned Highnesse OLIVER Late LORD PROTECTOR of this Common-Wealth, &c.”

The danger isn't the absence of money. It's that work was the architecture and now you can feel the load-bearing walls were temporary.

Knowing the heart of man is set to be / The centre of his world, about the which / These reuolutions of disturbances / Still roule — and that vnlesse aboue himselfe he can / Erect himselfe, how poore a thing is man? — Daniel

Samuel Daniel, “TO THE LADY MARGARET COVNTESSE OF CVM”

The boring reading: this is just desire. The real one: it's prior to desire. You're not asking for anything to happen. You're naming proximity itself as the thing.

For as thy absence doth excell / All distance known: / So doth thy nearenesse bear the bell, / Making two one. — Herbert

George Herbert, “The Search.”

The strategy that fails on every metric gets reclassified as a strategy that hasn't been understood yet. The mind that produced the disaster becomes the only mind qualified to interpret it.

in failure and mistake, / Relinquishment, disgrace, on every side,— / Merely examples for his sake, / Helps to his path untried — Browning

Robert Browning, “VI READING A BOOK, UNDER THE CLIFF”

The book didn't need to deliver. It needed to exist long enough for people to reorganise their commitments around it. After that, the evidence is beside the point — you're not defending the book, you're defending the version of yourself that was right to believe it.

"A vain person may have so habitually indulged the wish, and persevered in the attempt, to appear what he is not, as to become himself one of his own proselytes" — Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge, “CHAPTER II”

The policy costs more than it saves but that's the point — the cost IS the curriculum. The lesson being taught is that lessons must be taught, regardless of whether they teach anything.

Man must do well out of a good intent; / Not for the servile feare of punishment. — Herrick

Robert Herrick, “Feare.”

The pattern repeats because the departure is the only part of becoming that has a shape. What comes after is slower, messier, and resists narrative. We keep telling the leaving because we still don't know how to tell the arriving.

Young, all lay in dispute; I shall know, being old. — Browning

Robert Browning, “Rabbi Ben Ezra”

The argument is "this benefits you personally" — but that's exactly the argument that fails, because the refusal was never about benefit. It was about who you'd have to share a system with. Pride that would rather sit in traffic than admit the bus works.

Woe unto those that drunk with self-conceit, / Value their own designs at such a rate / Which humane wisdome cannot reach; that sit / Enthron'd, as sole Monopolists of wit — King

Henry King, “The Woes of Esay.”

Woolf locates the difficulty of modern poetry not in technique but in timing. The old poem matches a feeling you already had; you hum along. The new poem is making the feeling now, in you, and you can't hum what's still being composed. Recognition requires a delay the present won't grant.

"The living poets express a feeling that is actually being made and torn out of us at the moment. One does not recognize it in the first place; often for some reason one fears it" — Woolf

Virginia Woolf, “A Room of One's Own”
